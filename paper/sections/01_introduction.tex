\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A deployed LLM may be asked to remove private, copyrighted, or hazardous
information long after training. Retraining for every request is usually
impractical, motivating approximate machine unlearning
\cite{cao2015towards,bourtoule2021machine,liu2024rethinking}. Its contract
is selective: forget the requested information while preserving knowledge
and capabilities outside the request.

This contract is commonly judged with aggregate forgetting and retention
scores. Even multi-axis evaluations summarize retained populations at the
model or checkpoint level. Aggregation enables comparison but hides the
distribution of damage: two models with similar retained utility may
degrade mildly and broadly or fail sharply on a coherent tail. Fine-grained
audits expose such localized failures, but mainly after unlearning or within
predefined neighborhoods
\cite{chang2025retain,cheng2026entanglement,ko2025holes}.

The apparent strongest method also changes with the objective, benchmark,
metric aggregation, and model-selection rule, while different objectives
distribute interference differently
\cite{thaker2024position,dorna2025openunlearning,wang2025gradient}. A
deployer therefore needs a prospective answer: for a fixed model, deletion
request, and unlearning regime, which retained behaviors are most likely to
fail, and where should a limited audit or protection budget be spent?

Existing prospective work already audits set pairs or ranks non-target
concepts; our distinction is a checkpoint-derived, two-signal score for each
retained behavior that is carried into a separate protection test
\cite{su2026preunlearn,anonymous2026neighbors}.

We formulate this need as \emph{prospective retained-behavior
susceptibility}: a per-behavior risk profile computed from the current
checkpoint before the target trajectory. The profile is a layer around an
existing unlearner, not another unlearning objective. Different readouts
motivate two non-exclusive failure geometries: local sensitivity to parameter
motion and proximity to the forget region in representation space. We measure
both for every parent and select their relative weight on target-free
development requests. Readout-wise patterns are interpreted descriptively;
they do not route a component off or determine its weight.

We define \emph{loss-shake energy} as a shared finite-difference estimate of
classical block-local gradient energy. It averages squared symmetric loss
responses to small random parameter shakes and is direction-agnostic. Reusing
each shake scores a candidate pool with batched forwards, without reverse mode
per candidate or stored per-example gradients. A forward-only hidden-state
neighbor score supplies the representation component.

We freeze both component scores before each target trajectory and organize
the evaluation around three separable questions. First, does the joint
profile predict per-behavior and tail damage on repair-held-out behaviors
better than either component alone? Second, is loss-shake faithful to exact gradient
energy, and does it add predictive value beyond proximity? Third, does the
frozen profile allocate a fixed protection budget better than predeclared
controls under common forgetting and utility constraints? Objective-wise component patterns
are explanatory; only paired improvement over both endpoints establishes
two-signal complementarity. Likewise, predictive ranking is not assumed to
guarantee effective repair.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Candidate-level risk.} We evaluate
    per-behavior and tail susceptibility before independently configured
    target trajectories.
    \item \textbf{Two-signal profile.} We combine
    forget-set proximity with candidate-side reverse-mode-free loss-shake
    scoring.
    \item \textbf{Probe to protection.} We measure
    parent-specific prediction behavior and probe fidelity and cost,
    with a constraint-matched protection comparison.
\end{itemize}
