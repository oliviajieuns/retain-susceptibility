
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asked why no pre-unlearning signal has consistently predicted
where unlearning damages retained behavior, and answered that the question
was ill-posed: susceptibility is not a model-intrinsic scalar but is
conditional on the damage channel that the unlearning objective induces.
The channel is declared in advance by where the objective's loss reads the
model -- outputs or representations -- and each channel is read by a
different predictor family. A balanced roster of objectives on both
channels exhibits the predicted interaction: gradient-magnitude probes win
on the output channel and go dark on the representation channel, while
representation-proximity probes show the mirrored profile.

We made the gradient channel's signal affordable with a backward-free
randomized-sensitivity probe -- squared symmetric central differences
along a few shared random directions -- whose validity is certified by a
preregistered fidelity gate, and we made the prediction actionable by
routing a fixed protection budget through the channel-matched probe into
guarded repair. In the preregistered crossed design, matched protection
reduced sealed-audit tail damage at matched forgetting for both parents,
while mismatched, random, and no protection did not.

The contribution is a prediction-and-protection layer, not another
unlearning objective: given any parent unlearner, read its channel, probe
cheaply, protect where the matched probe points. Its central implication
is that retain-side risk work should stop searching for the one true
susceptibility score -- and start conditioning on the channel.
