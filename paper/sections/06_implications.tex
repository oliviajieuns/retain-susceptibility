\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:implications}

\paragraph{Why loss-shake energy?}
For a small update in parameter block $\mathcal B$, the retained loss changes
to first order as
$\ell_x(\theta_0+\Delta\theta)-\ell_x(\theta_0)
\approx\nabla_{\mathcal B}\ell_x(\theta_0)^\top
\Delta\theta_{\mathcal B}$. Alignment with one anticipated forget update is
therefore fragile when the realized direction varies across parents and steps
\cite{wang2025gradient}. Loss-shake energy instead uses shared symmetric
shakes to estimate gradient energy for the whole candidate pool, without
candidate-side reverse mode or choosing a future direction. The identity is
classical; the new role is pre-target pool profiling. This signal asks which
losses are easy to disturb, not which are exposed to the current deletion
request. Forget-conditioned proximity supplies that second cue. Their mixture
is a practical risk profile, not a causal decomposition.

\paragraph{Why predict and protect?}
Prediction and protection answer different questions. Prediction tests
whether a weight frozen on target-free development requests anticipates
candidate-level and tail damage from independent target trajectories. A good
ranking alone, however, does not improve retention. Protection therefore uses
a separately frozen weight from the same signal family to allocate a fixed
post-unlearning guarded-repair budget, with transfer measured on a sealed
audit. Active allocation arms share the entry checkpoint, repair operator,
budget, and predeclared forgetting/utility contract; only the selector
changes. Random and single-component comparisons thus isolate allocation,
whereas no repair tests the full repair pipeline. The framework does not claim
that one predictor causally produces protection. It gives one pre-update
signal family two independently tested uses: prospective risk localization
and targeted resource allocation around existing unlearners.
